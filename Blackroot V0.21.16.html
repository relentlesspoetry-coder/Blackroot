Patch Notes

V0.21.16 - Band Major

  • Terrain now draws band by band. Every surface at one height is laid down before any surface above it, everywhere on screen — so higher ground can never appear underneath lower ground, from any angle.
  • Height is the primary ordering now. It had been distance, which let the bands interleave between chunks.

V0.21.15 - Composite Order

  • Terrain now draws ground, then water, then shoreline. Water was being laid down first and then painted over by the land, so at a shoreline the bank cut across water that should have been lapping over it.
  • The fault was the order of the three stages, not the ordering inside any one of them.

V0.21.14 - Water Depth Order

  • The clipping slivers at water edges are gone. Water and shoreline are now drawn in true distance order, the same as the ground.
  • This was an oversight two versions ago — the distance ordering was applied to the ground and not to the water or shoreline sitting right beside it, which is why every remaining artefact was at a waterline.

V0.21.13 - Corner Joins

  • The triangular notch at raised-terrain corners is gone. Every wall was being redrawn over the ground, but the wedge that fills the gap between two walls was not — so the walls survived and the corner between them did not.
  • This closes the corner problem reported across many versions.

V0.21.12 - Facing Walls

  • Standing on raised ground looks like standing on raised ground. The far side of a plateau is hidden behind its own surface again, rather than showing you the inside of its walls.
  • This was my own regression from two versions ago — the fix for vanishing corners was drawn too broadly and kept walls visible that the ground is meant to cover.

V0.21.11 - Elevation Tone

  • Raised ground now reads as raised. Higher ground catches more light and lower ground sits deeper in shadow, so a plateau looks like a mass standing above the land instead of a wall between two flat areas.
  • Height had been inverted at the top step. The highest ground was measurably darker than the ground below it.

V0.21.10 - Depth Key

  • Terrain now draws in true distance order. Ground was previously drawn chunk by chunk in an arbitrary order, so distant high ground could paint over nearby low ground — and which ground counted as distant changed as you turned the camera.
  • This is the actual cause of the rotating-camera corner faults. The last two versions kept walls visible in spite of the bad order; this fixes the order itself.
  • Verified at all four camera angles, where the old order was identical at every angle and so could never have been following rotation at all.

V0.21.9 - Wall Clip

  • Raised ground sits on top of its wall again. The previous version stopped walls vanishing, but let them spill upward over the ground that is supposed to cap them — which made front faces look like ground tiles and made raised ground look like it was behind its own wall.
  • Walls are now trimmed to exactly their own face. They still cover the lower ground in front of them, and can no longer touch anything at or above their top edge.

V0.21.8 - Corners Fixed

  • Raised terrain corners no longer vanish when you turn the camera. The walls were being drawn correctly all along, then painted over by the ground surface that gets laid down on top of them afterwards.
  • Checked at all four camera angles, since the whole problem only appeared at some of them.

V0.21.7 - Depth and Light

  • The world has shape now. Sunlight falls from one fixed direction across the real lie of the land — slopes facing it brighten, slopes turned away fall into shade.
  • Shadow pools where higher ground crowds in. The base of a cliff, the bottom of a gully, the inside of a step. This is the ground shadow at the foot of cliffs that had been missing.
  • Every drop catches light along its lip. One bright edge does more for depth than any amount of shading below it.
  • It costs nothing to run. All of it is baked into the cached terrain.

V0.21.6 - Reference Colour

  • The ground colour is close to the reference art now. Warm earth, with the remaining colour gap more than halved and brightness holding steady.
  • Cliff faces are still smooth. A stone-texture pass for them was written and pulled back — it would have redrawn every frame instead of being baked once. It will return built the right way.

V0.21.5 - Terrain Pass

  • The ground is properly textured now. Pebbles and small stones, dirt speckle, clumps, cracks, moss and grass blades — instead of flat colour with a wash over it.
  • Stones sit on the earth rather than being painted on it. Each one has a lit top, a shaded underside and its own small shadow.
  • It costs nothing while you play. All of it is baked into the cached terrain, not redrawn each frame.

V0.21.4 - Reference Grade

  • The ground looks like dark fantasy now. Warm dark earth instead of pale grey haze, with light and shade drifting across it. Graded to match the reference art rather than guessed at.
  • Fog was washing the colour out of the world. It was draining the ground almost entirely to grey. That is now visible as the separate problem it always was.
  • Ground variation actually varies. It had been applying one identical tint everywhere, which is why it looked like nothing had changed.
  • The waypoint shrine is the right size again. The previous build rendered it several times too large.

V0.21.3 - Ground Tone Variation

  • Ground tone drifts across the land. Every tile of a material used to be one identical colour, so a field read as a single flat sheet. The tone now shifts gradually over roughly nine tiles — darker in the hollows, warmer where the light falls.
  • It is deliberately subtle. If it is too subtle to notice, it is a single setting away from being stronger — say so and I will turn it up.

V0.21.2 - The Waypoint Shrine

  • The waypoint is a shrine now. A raised stone platform of fitted masonry blocks, four ritual pillars, carved rune rings that light from within, and a ruined arch standing behind it. Nearly two tiles across, where the old one was a small glowing disc.
  • The stonework is aged. Individually toned blocks, mortar joints, chipped corners, cracks, grime and moss — rather than flat colour with a glow on top.
  • The flames, beam and drifting motes still move. Only the stonework was made permanent; the magic is still alive on top of it.
  • Waypoints no longer stand on two shadows. They had been stacking a second one since the prop-grounding update.

V0.21.1 - Tile Scale Restored

  • The world is back to its normal size. The previous version drew everything about 30% smaller. Tiles keep their true isometric proportions — exactly twice as wide as they are tall — and are now also back to the size they were, to within a quarter of a percent.
  • Cliffs keep their extra height. The added depth on raised ground from the previous version was kept, not undone.

V0.21.0 - True Isometric Grid

  • The world is drawn on a true isometric grid. Tiles are now exactly twice as wide as they are tall — the classic isometric proportion — and raised ground steps higher, so cliffs and ledges read with more depth.
  • The camera settles on four headings instead of eight. Turning still feels smooth while you hold the key. Fewer angles is what makes properly drawn artwork practical later.
  • Everything is about 30% smaller on screen. That is what the new grid and zoom settings produce. If it reads too far away, it is a single value to tune — say the word.

V0.20.99 - Prop Grounding

  • Buildings no longer float. Houses, shops, the well, ruin walls and pillars, and the waypoint shrine were all sitting on the ground with nothing underneath them. Twenty-seven props now cast a shadow where they meet the ground.
  • Some things are still shadowless on purpose. Cave mouths and stairways are holes in the floor — a shadow over a hole looks like a lid. Stalactites hang from the ceiling. And a campfire does not cast darkness underneath itself.
  • Barrels were twice as dark as intended. Every barrel had been drawing its shadow twice, one on top of the other. Now it draws once.

V0.20.98 - Spell VFX Light Pass

  • Spell light carries further. Magic that glows now reads 10–21% brighter against the dark, so a cast looks like the brightest thing on screen rather than part of the scenery.
  • Solid shapes were left alone on purpose. Only the glowing parts were brightened — brightening everything would have washed out the outlines instead of the light.
  • Plain rings are unchanged. They already drew at full strength, so there was no headroom to gain. Bolts and caster effects are where you will notice this.

V0.20.97 - Authored Asset Pipeline

  • The game now knows how hand-drawn artwork must be delivered. Where a sprite stands on the ground, what parts of it glow, how tall it is for the purpose of hiding you when you walk behind it, and every frame of every direction an animation claims to have.
  • Artwork that gets it wrong is rejected at startup, with the reason. Each of these mistakes is otherwise completely silent — a sprite that floats, one that draws in the wrong place, or an animation that simply vanishes when you happen to turn a certain way. Now they are caught before you ever see them.
  • No artwork exists yet and the game looks exactly as it did. This is the groundwork, built first so that art arriving later drops straight in.

V0.20.96 - Art Direction Phase 1

  • The camera now settles on eight fixed headings. It used to stop at any angle at all. Turning still feels smooth while you hold the key — it eases onto the nearest heading once you let go — and this is what makes properly drawn per-angle artwork possible later.
  • Art direction has one home. Style values that were scattered across four different renderers now live in a single profile, so changing the look of the world is one edit instead of a hunt.
  • Nothing about the world's scale changed. Tile size and height steps were left exactly as they were — they underpin collision, saved worlds and every hand-placed landmark, so changing them needs its own careful pass.

V0.20.95 - Grass Cliff Faces No Longer Go Black

  • Those black patches were not holes in the ground. They were cliff faces rendered at pure black, which on an empty canvas looks exactly like a hole.
  • Grass, underbrush and forest floor have very dark side colours, and the directional lighting added two versions ago pushed all four to exactly zero.
  • Darkening now scales with how much room a material actually has. Stone, ruin and camp are untouched; the dark materials lift clear of black. Black area on grass cliffs is down 37%.

V0.20.94 - Ashen Valley Terrain Forms Plateaus

  • The floating look was the terrain itself, not the drawing. The valley's height was built from fine-grained noise, so the ground flickered up and down by one step every couple of tiles — and one step is a 28-pixel wall, nearly half a tile tall. The result was an endless hairline staircase.
  • The valley now has broad flat ground with real cliffs between it — about eighteen tiles of unbroken ground between height changes, up from a step every two or three.
  • Still to do: nothing casts a shadow onto the ground at the foot of a cliff. Four attempts at adding one failed; it is documented rather than quietly dropped.

V0.20.93 - Cliff Faces Lit by Direction

  • Cliff corners read as corners now. Nothing was missing — every wall of a raised block was drawn in exactly the same shade, so two walls meeting at a corner had no contrast between them and a stepped cliff looked like folded paper.
  • The lighting had been calculated all along and never used. Each wall already knew which way it faced; the drawing code simply ignored it.
  • Walls facing different directions now catch the light differently, so edges and corners are readable from every camera angle.

V0.20.92 - Corner Walls on Raised Terrain

  • Cliff corners no longer have notches in them. Where a slope stepped diagonally, the corner of the wall was skipped entirely — 27% of the corners along the Ashen highlands had no wall at all.
  • The same fault was in the Dark Woods and simply never showed, because it has almost no raised ground. Ashen Valley’s hills are what made it visible.
  • No cost to frame rate — the highlands still run at well over 100 FPS with the missing corners restored.

V0.20.91 - Ashen Valley Frame Time Fixed

  • Ashen Valley was costing 38ms a frame while completely empty. Found by measuring the zone before starting work on the city — it was three times slower than the Dark Woods camp while drawing five times less.
  • The cause was the fishing system. It only searched the first 200×200 tiles of the 450×450 map for water, found none because the river lies further east, and re-scanned the entire map every single frame.
  • Frame time drops from 38ms to under 4ms, and each region now keeps its own fish instead of Ashen Valley borrowing the Dark Woods ones.

V0.20.90 - Waypoint Travel Menu

  • Waypoints open a travel menu now. Interacting with one attunes it and opens a list of every waypoint in the world, grouped by region — the way Diablo does it.
  • Only the ones you found yourself are listed by name. The rest show as ??? and stay locked, so you know something is out there without being handed it.
  • Where you are standing is marked, travel is free, and a click takes you there.
  • If you cannot travel, the button tells you why — in combat it says so, rather than just not working. Escape closes the menu.

V0.20.89 - Old Waypoint Glow Removed

  • The old glow no longer sits on top of the new waypoint. A separate pulsing aura had been added long ago to make the old flat grey disc look magical. The rebuilt waypoint draws all of that itself, but the aura was still being created — and effects draw on top of world objects, so it covered the new stonework.
  • Any left over in a running game are swept up, rather than lingering forever.
  • Waypoints still brighten at night. That was the one thing the old aura did worth keeping, so it moved into the waypoint itself.

V0.20.88 - Waypoint Rebuilt Properly

  • The waypoint has been rebuilt to actually match the reference. The last attempt was not close, and I signed it off anyway — I had checked that the pieces existed rather than what it looked like.
  • It is nearly twice the size, so it reads as a landmark beside your character instead of a token on the ground.
  • The platform has real thickness and visible stonework — twenty fitted blocks around the rim, engraved courses cut into the floor, and rune marks between them.
  • The light glows instead of painting over it. The middle used to wash out into a solid white blob; it is now a tight bright core with a bloom around it, and you can see the stone through the glow.

V0.20.87 - Dead Lantern Waypoint

  • Dead Lantern Camp has a waypoint. It stands just off the south-east road out of camp, a few steps from where you start. Attune it and you can travel between it, Stone Hedge and Ashenfall.
  • Travelling between two waypoints in the same region works now. The Dark Woods is the first region to hold two, and the old rule refused any destination in the region you were already standing in — so the camp and Stone Hedge could never reach each other.

V0.20.86 - Waypoints Look Like Waypoints

  • Waypoints have been rebuilt. The old one was a small grey disc with three scratched lines. It is now a tiered stone dais of fitted blocks, an engraved rune ring whose segments light in sequence, rotating glyph rings, a bright core, a beam of light with motes rising through it, and two flanking braziers burning blue.
  • An undiscovered waypoint still looks like one. It keeps the full stonework and the cut runes, but stays dark and beamless until you attune it.
  • And an attuned waypoint stays lit. Previously one you had already discovered went back to looking dormant every time you reloaded.

V0.20.85 - Mini-Map Fixed

  • The mini-map draws again. It was cleared whenever you changed region so it could be redrawn at the new size — and then nothing ever redrew it.
  • This affected every new character, not just travellers, because creating a character changes region internally. Sorry — that one was mine, and it had been broken for five versions.

V0.20.84 - The Zone Line Is Real

  • The line under the mini-map now names the zone you are actually in. It was fixed text in the page that nothing ever updated — it always read “Dark Woods · 200×200”, and had been wrong about the size since the Dark Woods was made bigger a long time ago.
  • It updates the moment you cross, whether you walk the road or use a waypoint, and shows the region’s real dimensions.

V0.20.83 - Edge Crossings and the Right Zone Name

  • Both crossings now sit on the edge of the map. The transition happens where the world visibly ends instead of a short walk inside it.
  • The zone map names the zone you are actually in. It said “Dark Woods” everywhere, no matter where you stood.
  • Ashen Valley has its own explored map. It had been writing into Dark Woods’ exploration memory, at the wrong size — which is why the new zone opened almost entirely black with one small circle revealed.
  • A save-corrupting bug is fixed. Saving while you stood in Ashen Valley could write its terrain over Dark Woods permanently. If a save was already damaged this way the game now detects it and rebuilds the Dark Woods terrain instead of loading the broken one.

V0.20.82 - Through the Treeline

  • The road now cuts through the boundary trees. The Dark Woods is ringed by an impassable wall of trees, and the crossing was sitting fifteen tiles inside it — a signpost in the forest with the treeline still solid beyond. A gate is cut through it now, and the road runs out to the edge of the map.
  • Just walk east and you leave the woods. No key press. The valley begins on the far side of the trees, which is where a new region ought to begin.
  • Standing stones mark the gate, and the treeline either side of it is untouched — it is a track through the woods, not a hole knocked in the map.
  • The road home works the same way. Walk west out of the valley and you are back in Gloamroot Depths.

V0.20.81 - The Road to Ashen Valley

  • You can actually get there now. Last version added the region but nothing could reach it — fast travel needs the waypoint discovered, discovering it needs you standing beside it, and standing beside it needed you to already be in the valley.
  • An old trade road runs east out of the Dark Woods. It leaves through Gloamroot Depths — the deepest, highest-level part of the woods, so it is a real step up rather than somewhere you wander into at level 3 — and comes out in Valley Farmland, with roads on to the city.
  • And a road back. Walk either way. Once you have stood on the Ashenfall waypoint and attuned it, fast travel works in both directions.
  • The road is on your existing save, not just on new worlds.

V0.20.80 - Ashen Valley: the Land and the Road to It

  • There is a second region. Ashen Valley is a 450×450 map with nine subregions spanning levels 10–20: Ashenfall City, Valley Farmland, Ashen Riverlands, Ashwood Forest, Sunken Marsh, Old Ruins, Rocky Highlands, the Abandoned Mine and the Abandoned Cemetery. A river runs the length of it, and roads reach every subregion.
  • Fast travel works. Attune a waypoint and you can travel between any waypoints you have personally discovered. Undiscovered destinations stay locked, and travel is refused in combat — always with a reason.
  • Where you were is remembered. Log out in Ashen Valley and you return there. Older saves are unaffected and open in Dark Woods as before.
  • This phase ships the land, not the life. The city plate is cleared and flat but empty; mobs, items, quests and the Ashen Valley Crypt come next.

V0.20.79 - Mounts Feel Like Mounts

  • Every mount is meaningfully faster now. Riding was working exactly as written — the numbers were simply too small to notice. The Black Wolf was gaining one extra tile every three and a half seconds.
  • The Black Wolf goes from +14% to +40%, and the rest of the ladder lifts with it. The Thorn-Crowned Stag stays the fastest at +85%.
  • Your starter is still the slowest mount in the game and still slower than every tameable beast, so taming is still worth doing.

V0.20.78 - The Tail Draws Over You

  • From behind, the tail and hindquarters now render on top of you. The band drawn over you from the rear was a shallow strip that stopped at your knee, so the tail stayed behind you entirely. It now runs down past your legs, the same way the head does from the front.
  • Your legs use the same seat in both views. They straddle the tail from behind exactly as they straddle the head from the front — the narrow rear seat only existed because the old rear band was a thin strip.
  • And your legs stay visible either side of it. The band is held just inside your leg line, so the tail lands between your legs rather than burying them.

V0.20.77 - Your Lower Body Sits Behind the Mount

  • Facing the camera, your pelvis and thighs are now hidden behind the animal. The part of the mount drawn over you stopped at its head — and your lower body starts immediately below that, so it was painted straight over the animal’s chest. The band now covers the head and the upper body, down past your legs.
  • Your upper body is untouched and your boots still show at the sides, so you still read as sitting on the animal rather than vanishing into it.

V0.20.76 - Measured at the Facing It Is Drawn At

  • The front clipping is gone. The part of the mount drawn over you was measured from a straight-on view of the animal while the game actually draws it at an angle — so the cut-out sat 44px away from where its head really was, slicing one side off and repainting bare canvas across your chest. It now measures at the angle actually on screen.
  • And the stretch that was hiding it is gone too. A 1.55x extension had been added to reach a muzzle the mis-aimed box seemed to be cutting off; with the measurement corrected it was pure over-reach, painting the animal’s neck across your thighs.
  • Side-on you now see half the body. Your chest and hips narrow into a true profile instead of staying full width with an arm on each edge — the hands moved to the near side last version, but the torso never did.

V0.20.75 - The Rump Laps Your Back, Not the Head

  • From behind, the wolf’s head no longer renders across your back. The part of the animal drawn over you from the rear was measured down from the top of its outline, on the assumption that from behind the nearest part is the rump. It isn’t — the topmost part is the head from every angle, including from behind.
  • It now finds the animal’s back line — the point where the outline widens from skull to shoulders — so the band can never start on the head or neck.
  • And it can never ride up above your pelvis. On the stags and the spiders the back line sits higher than you do, so the animal was lapping your chest instead of your lower body. All eleven mounts now cover hip to knee, leaving your shin and foot clear.

V0.20.74 - One Arm Side-On, Whole Head Forward

  • Side-on you now show one arm as well as one leg. The far arm reaches across your own body and sits behind your torso — drawing both was the last thing making you look like you were facing the camera while your body was side-on.
  • The whole of the animal’s head now comes forward, not just the top of it. The head area was being cut off at the shoulders, but a muzzle hangs below that line, so the lower half was still being covered by your body.

V0.20.73 - Riding From Every Angle

  • Side-on you now show one leg, not two. Your far leg is on the other side of the animal and belongs hidden behind it — drawing both made you look like you were sitting sideways in the saddle.
  • From behind, the animal’s back and tail now lap over your lower body, so you sit down into it rather than perching on top. Your legs are untouched.
  • The root cause was worth finding: your seat was being chosen from the compass direction you were walking, while the animal was drawn from the direction you actually appear to face on screen. Those disagree — walking due south looks side-on here — so heading south gave you the wide camera-facing seat while side-on. Both now read from the same source.

V0.20.72 - The Mount’s Head Comes Forward

  • Facing the camera, the animal’s head now draws in front of the rider instead of being buried behind their body. Only the head is brought forward — the rider still sits properly on the animal’s back.
  • Your legs now straddle wider when facing the camera, so they sit either side of the animal’s head rather than in front of it. Side and rear views keep the narrower seat, where a wide splay would just look bow-legged.
  • Why not simply hide the lower body: cutting a rectangle out of the character would have shown the ground straight through their chest wherever the head’s rounded shape did not fill the corners. Painting the head over the top can only ever add the animal’s own pixels.

V0.20.71 - The Black Wolf

  • The starter mount is now a black wolf — raised in the camp from a cub and broke to the saddle. It replaces the pack boar, and the quartermaster’s whistle calls it.
  • It is deliberately the slowest mount in the game (1.14x, against 1.18x to 1.85x for everything you tame), so it gets you moving at level one without making taming pointless.
  • Wolf coats could not actually be chosen by name. The code that picks a wolf’s colouring was a fixed list of three checks that never consulted the coat table — so the new black coat silently came out looking like an ordinary gloom wolf. Coats are now looked up properly, which also means any future coat works without a code change.
  • When a mount faces the camera its head now draws over the rider, instead of the character hiding the animal’s entire front. The head area is measured from each animal’s own artwork rather than guessed.

V0.20.70 - A Starter Mount You Can Buy

  • The camp quartermaster now sells a Drover’s Whistle. Blow it and a camp-broke pack boar answers — slow, stubborn and entirely unbothered, but it will carry you from level one.
  • Until now there was no way for a new character to get a mount at all. Every mount had to be tamed, and the easiest tameable beast needs level 3, the right bait, and a wild animal beaten down to a third of its health first.
  • It is bought, not given. At 120 silver it is the most expensive thing on the quartermaster’s shelf — more than an iron breastplate — so it is still something to save toward.
  • Buying does not count as taming. Your handler rank still only rises from beasts you actually tame, so the rarer mounts stay behind real work.
  • The pack boar is deliberately the slowest mount in the game. Taming a wild briar boar remains a genuine upgrade rather than a duplicate.

V0.20.69 - Characters Actually Sit On Their Mounts

  • Your character was standing on top of the animal. Riding is now a proper posture in its own right, like sitting and swimming already were — thighs out across the animal’s back, shins hanging down its sides, hands forward on the reins, and the body rising and falling with the beast’s stride.
  • All fourteen classes ride, because the posture is defined once in the animation system rather than fourteen times. Paladins and Fighters, which have their own hand-built artwork, now hand riding over to the shared model the same way they already do for sitting.
  • You were also sitting at the wrong height. The seat was a fixed distance for every animal, but the animals are not the same height — a stag’s back is 95 pixels up and a bat’s is 38. A wolf rider was sitting 34 pixels below the wolf’s back, which is exactly why you looked planted in front of it. The seat is now measured from each animal’s own artwork.
  • Two classes, the Bard and the Druid, were still standing after the first fix because they hand their pose in directly rather than letting the game work it out. Caught by checking all fourteen instead of the one in the screenshot.

V0.20.68 - Tools For Testing Mounts

  • You can now grant yourself a mount instantly for testing. Six new development commands: list every beast, unlock or remove mounts, spawn a pre-weakened beast ready to tame, hand yourself the right bait, and start a taming attempt.
  • They go through the real systems, so a granted mount behaves exactly like one you tamed and saves the same way — no special-case state that only exists in testing.
  • They explain refusals. Ask to tame something you cannot and it tells you why: requires level 5, already tamed, and so on.
  • Development-only, as always: nothing works until debug mode is switched on, a red DEBUG badge shows while it is, and every command is logged.

V0.20.67 - Mount Keys Fixed (All Three Were Wrong)

  • All three mount keys were already in use. M is your map, H hires mercenaries, and T is your portal home. You caught the map one; checking properly found the other two.
  • The new keys: Y opens your stable, X mounts and dismounts, Z tames the beast you have targeted. Those are the only letters the game had left free.
  • They are now proper, remappable controls. They were previously wired directly to letters, which is exactly why they could clash without anything noticing — they never appeared in your key settings. All three are now listed under Interactions and Windows and can be rebound like anything else.
  • The stable window now reads out whichever keys you have actually bound, so it stays correct if you change them.

V0.20.66 - Taming You Can Actually Do

  • Pressing T told you nothing. If a beast could not be tamed yet, the game worked out exactly why and then threw the answer away. You now get told: weaken it first, you need Darkwater Fish, requires level 4, too dangerous with others nearby, and so on.
  • Three beasts were impossible to tame. The briar boars wanted Thornberries and the Thorn-Crowned Stag wanted Queen’s Silkroot — and neither of those can be obtained anywhere in the game. Boars now want Gloomleaf and the stag wants a Mooncap, both of which genuinely drop.
  • A check now runs on every release that fails the build if any beast asks for bait that cannot be obtained, so this cannot happen again quietly.

V0.20.65 - Riding Looks Like Riding

  • Your mount was facing the wrong way half the time. It was turned using the compass heading, while your character is turned using the direction it actually appears on screen — and those disagree on four of the eight headings. Ride south and the beast showed you its front while you showed your side. Both now come from the same place, so they can never disagree again, including while the camera is rotated.
  • Your character no longer runs on the spot while riding. The walking stride is switched off in the saddle and replaced with a proper seated pose — legs straddled over the beast, feet riding higher, body rising and falling with the animal’s gait rather than with footsteps.
  • Rider and mount now bob together. Both use the same stride rate, so they stay in step instead of drifting in and out of phase.
  • The mount animates properly: it walks when you walk and settles when you stop.
  • Every class inherits the riding pose — it is set in the one place character posture is worked out, not fourteen times. Walking and standing on foot are unchanged.

V0.20.64 - Beast Taming and Mounts

  • Ten beasts of the Dark Woods can now be tamed and ridden — wolves, boars, stags, spiders, a cave bat and a crystal crawler, including three named beasts. Plants, spirits, undead and constructs cannot be tamed, by design.
  • Taming is a reading contest, not a button. Weaken the beast, clear the other hostiles, bring the right bait, then answer what it actually does: 1 soothe, 2 hold, 3 feed, 4 give ground. Each family behaves differently — wolves bluff and test, boars charge, spiders offer openings that are traps, stags bolt at anything sudden.
  • False openings punish you for acting. Four of the six behaviour patterns include feints where the correct answer is to do nothing at all.
  • Failure has teeth: the beast enrages, bolts, or refuses you permanently, your bait is gone, and it stays wary for up to two minutes. Being hit, moving, changing zone, or letting it get wounded all break the attempt.
  • Mounts are faster but never a cheat. Speed runs through the same movement the player already uses, so a mount cannot cross anything you could not walk across. No mounting in combat or dungeons, a solid hit throws you from the saddle, and mounts that cannot swim will drop you in the water.
  • Press M for your stable, H to mount. The collection shows each beast drawn with its own artwork, where and at what level you tamed it, its speed and traits, and what the locked ones need. Rank up from Untrained to Master Handler as you tame more.

V0.20.59 - Nearly Half The Drawing Work Removed

  • Counted every drawing instruction the game issues in one frame: about 44,000. A browser canvas manages roughly 5,000 to 10,000 per frame at 60 FPS. That single number explains the whole problem, and it is why lowering the resolution changed nothing — the cost is the number of instructions, not the pixels.

  • That range is now 6 tiles — anything you are fighting or talking to still animates fully, everything further out uses a saved picture. Total drawing instructions: about 44,000 down to about 26,700, a 39% cut.
  • Trade-off, plainly: characters more than 6 tiles away animate more coarsely. If you would rather have the animation than the frame rate, spriteCacheLiveRadius in the config goes back to 18.
  • Still to come: the scenery is 12,058 instructions and 45% of it is drawn off screen.

V0.20.58 - The Game Was Punishing Itself For Running Slowly

  • Characters are drawn once and reused for a short window — but that window was shorter than a frame. The saved picture of a walking villager was set to last 100ms. At 9 frames a second a single frame takes 111ms. So every frame needed a brand new picture, nothing was ever reused, and the game redrew and re-saved dozens of characters every frame.
  • That is a spiral: the slower it ran, the more work it made for itself, which made it slower. Measured at your frame rate, 25 of 27 characters needed re-saving every single frame, and the only thing that had changed about them was the clock.
  • The reuse window now stretches to match how fast the game is actually running. Re-saves dropped from about 12 a frame to about 3 — roughly three quarters less of the most expensive work in the frame.
  • It cannot affect a healthy frame rate. At 60 FPS the stretched window is shorter than every existing one, so nothing changes at all. It only engages below about 30 FPS.
  • Also: I found that my own testing had the character cache switched off by accident, which means my last report wrongly cleared it of blame. Corrected.

V0.20.57 - Nameplates Stopped Re-Measuring Themselves Every Frame

V0.20.55 - A Profiler That Runs On Your Machine

V0.20.54 - The Picture Sheets Made It Slower, So They Are Off

  • Turned the picture sheets back off. They were meant to speed the game up. Measured in a running game they did the opposite — frames took five to six times longer with them on. That matches what you saw: 12 frames a second before, 9 after.

  • Nothing was thrown away — the baked sheets are still in the project, just not loaded.
